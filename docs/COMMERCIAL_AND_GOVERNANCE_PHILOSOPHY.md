# CTX Commercial and Governance Philosophy
If a language model and its agent lose context, this is the tool you need.

This document defines how CTX balances technical openness, project control, and future monetization, licensing, or acquisition.

## Goal

CTX aims to be:

- locally usable
- modifiable and extensible
- portable through the `.ctx` format
- commercializable as software
- protectable as a strategic asset

## Core principle

CTX should be open in practice, but governed in a centralized way.

That means:

- third parties can use it
- third parties can modify it
- third parties can redistribute under the project license
- but the official direction, brand, primary repository, and rights needed to relicense or sell must remain centralized

## Why this matters

If the project is legally fragmented across many copyright holders or diffused governance, it becomes hard to:

- relicense
- close commercial agreements
- sell the software
- bring the project into a company or acquire it

CTX needs operational openness without ownership fragmentation.

## Recommended governance model

### 1. A clear holder

The official project should have a single clear IP holder or a legal vehicle controlled by the holder.

Today:

- initial holder: Diego Mariano Verrastro

Recommended evolution:

- transfer official IP to a company when it makes commercial sense

### 2. Controlled official repository

There should be a single official repository whose strategic direction remains under the holder.

This includes:

- official roadmap
- official releases
- official licensing
- official brand

### 3. Contributions under assignment or CLA

External contributions should not enter "free" if the goal is to preserve the ability to relicense, sell, or restructure.

CTX should maintain:

- contributor assignment
- or a CLA with broad enough rights

### 4. Reserved brand

The `CTX` brand must remain separate from code usage permission.

This enables:

- forks: yes
- commercial confusion: no

## Commercial philosophy

The core business of CTX is software.

This implies:

- commercial local use allowed under license
- self-hosted deployment allowed
- support, integration, and customization allowed
- competing hosted services restricted without a separate license

## What CTX allows

- personal use
- professional use
- local enterprise use
- on-premise
- self-hosted
- modifications
- forks
- redistribution under the CTX license

## What CTX restricts

- offering CTX as a competing service
- presenting forks as the official distribution
- using the CTX brand in confusing ways
- relicensing the official distribution as if a third party were the holder

## Future acquisition

If a company buys CTX in the future, the asset value is not just code.

The value is:

- centralized copyright
- brand control
- official repository control
- clear licensing
- normalized contributions
- `.ctx` format associated with the official project
- community and adoption around the official distribution

## Conditions that favor a sale

To keep CTX acquisition-friendly, maintain:

- a clear ownership history
- consistent contribution agreements
- a license that allows the current model without blocking future relicensing
- a clear separation between the official project and forks
- a recognizable, defensible brand

## What to avoid

Avoid:

- binding community governance too early
- independent foundation in this stage
- contributions without assignment or CLA
- ambiguity about who can relicense
- multiple holders without a central agreement

## Recommended structure over time

Stage 1:

- project controlled by the founder
- clear source-available license
- regulated contributions

Stage 2:

- transfer IP to a company if the business matures
- formal commercial agreements
- more formalized brand

Stage 3:

- commercial distribution
- professional support
- enterprise
- potential strategic partnerships or acquisition

## Golden rule

CTX can be open in usage and modification.

But to be sellable in the future, ownership of the official project must not be diluted.
